 What is the difference between the tabular and the geospatial data?

The tabular data presents aggregated information per each central urban area and audited item. For example, If city center X scores 75% in the indicator titled “Graffiti: Yes”, this means that 75% of the total street-segments length have at least one graffiti present.

On the other hand, the pre-processed geospatial data grids illustrate average density (decile) maps (50m X 50m) of street- or crossing-segments per each audit item (in kilometers per square kilometers).

 Value of the Data General Although there is a significant policy interest for healthier, more sustainable, inclusive, and resilient transportation systems, there is a significant data scarcity in comparable and street-level walkability indicators. The lack of relevant indicators across Europe and places with heterogeneous characteristics constitutes an important barrier in the generalizability of walkability research findings. On this basis and to our knowledge for the first time, our dataset enables micro-level walkability assessments and comparisons among a diverse group of European downtown neighbourhoods and cities. Furthermore, it can assist urban areas facing significant pressures on their mobility systems to plan more appropriate strategies and deliver more walking-friendly and sustainable environments by benefitting from such a data-driven frameworks. Target Groups Decision makers, stakeholders and urban policy analysts from the following sectors can significantly benefit from the provided datasets: researchers, urban and transport planners, public health practitioners, decision makers and city councils, NGOs, think tanks, journalists. Resuability The reusability of the dataset can support the construction of composite indicators and city rankings related to urban sustainability issues. It also encourages cross-city and peer-city comparisons in pedestrian planning issues across Europe. Usage The entire geospatial dataset can additionally be utilized by local planners across Europe in order to identify hot/cold spots of low/high walkability patterns and accordingly to propose cost-effective and more realistic solutions and strategies. These data can greatly support cohesion policies targeting inclusive, healthy, and sustainable urban development for European cities by detecting significant disparities in their pedestrian environments. How data were acquired? We used a brief and validated street observation instrument [2–4] to assessed 17 micro-level walkability indicators for 59 European downtown districts. The complete audit item list is presented in the Appendix in Table A-1. In order to conduct the street audits we trained 46 observers and utilised the free online geospatial service of Google Street View (GSV). The collected block-by-block street ratings were digitized in polyline vectors and stored in a GIS database. Finally, segment-level GIS vector files were processed and analysed to formulate the above mentioned walkability indicators. Data format?

Spreadsheet: Processed/aggregated data tables in .xls format

In the Microsoft Excel Workbook (.xlsx) ‘WALKABILITY_DATA_BARTZOKAS-TSIOMPRAS_et.al_2021.xlsx’ the worksheet ‘total_descriptive_statistics’ presents some basic information by city with regard to the audited area size, the total number of audited street- and crossing-segments and the no data records of the primary GIS database. In the ‘GSV_stats’ worksheet rows demonstrate the share of audited segments in each central urban district while columns represent the year of Google Street View image used. Finally, the ‘data_indicators’ worksheet contains the summarized results of the auditing process per central urban area and per item included in the audit tool’s list. All data values in this table are expressed in weighted total shares of each downtown’s streets or crossings segments and all indicator columns refer to specific codes in brackets providing data reference information.

Geospatial: Processed/aggregated geospatial data in .shp or .geojson format

Our map provides open access to pre-processed geospatial data grids and in high spatial resolution (50 m X 50 m). The geospatial data of the platform include both the micro-level walkability indicators and the boundary polygons of each analyzed central urban area. The geospatial grids have been calculated using the Kernel density tool in ArcGIS Desktop, v.10.3 (ESRI, REDLANDS) applied to the raw GIS vectors (i.e., polylines), created during the street observation phase of our research. We decided to implement the specific spatial analysis process in order to smoothen the collected information in a way that will be easier to visualize and understand. Users can download either a selected indicator for a specific area or they can download all the spatial dataset of the Web-GIS platform.
The .zip file of the data also includes a read_me.txt file explaining the codes of each column in the table and a disclaimer.txt file.

 Parameters for data collection? The raw GIS data were collected from 59 central urban areas across Europe. Unfortunately, in this research we could not consider cities from the following EU countries: Germany, Finland, Malta, and Cyprus because either the Google Street View service was significantly outdated or the image coverage of their respective downtown streets was insufficient. The boundaries of the most central administrative districts of the above cities delineated our study areas (e.g., Madrid Centro, Oslo Centrum, Brussels Pentagon District, etc.). Whenever such a boundary was difficult to be identified, we calculated a 15 minutes walking distance service area polygon originating from the most popular and centrally located landmark in the city (e.g., Palatul Parlamentului in Bucharest). In a similar way, for cases where Google image data were missing or problematic (e.g., objects totally blocking the view of the sidewalk, blurred images, etc.) the corresponding footpaths were not rated. Description of data collection? Street-level data were collected between January 2019 and September 2020. The presented aggregated - secondary data indicators were pre-processed in October 2020. In practice, we adopted the brief version of the Microscale Audit of Pedestrian Streetscapes (MAPS-mini) to which we added two extra items. Namely, sidewalk width and number of traffic lanes. We opted to use the specific audit tool because it is both a validated instrument [2–5] as well as commonly used in physical activity and walkability studies. Furthermore, it is a brief but robust tool and thus suitable for massive data collection. The audit list included seventeen items related to micro-level characteristics of neighbourhood design, such as sidewalks, crosswalks, lights, curb cuts, street furniture, parks, transit, aesthetics. In practice, observers virtually completed the item-list by submitting his/her ratings in the GIS database. Each block-by-block recorded street observation was separately pertained to both sides of each street and crossing segment in the district. The application of the audit tool was conducted online, using the GSV service. On this basis, we trained 46 observers (i.e., undergraduate and postgraduate students) on how to implement the specific method. To ensure homogenous and robust ratings among the cities and the auditors, we also employed and trained a supervising team of four experienced researchers (i.e., PhD students or planning/architecture professionals). In essence, supervisors played the role of trainers, data quality auditors, and advisors accordingly and with respect to the observers. To this end, the supervising team validated data collection samples per each auditor periodically, proposed possible data quality improvements, and verified the auditors ability to follow the instructions and rules of the data collection process effectively.  What data indicators are available?

In this data hub users have access in 14 street-segment level indicators and 3 crossing-segment level indicators. All datasets are provided either in aggregated data tables or aggregated pre-processed shapefiles.

 Methodology: Raw data collection Virtual Walkability Audit Summary

Table 1 summarizes some fundamental characteristics of the conducted research during which we analyzed 59 downtown districts, from 26 European countries, audited 112.577 segments with a total length of 9.822 km, trained 46 observers, employed 4 Supervisors by utilizing Google Street View (GSV) imagery data. The time duration for the primary GIS data collection phase was 21 months (January 2019 - September 2020). The resulting aggregated data indicators can be useful to planners and decision makers interested micro-level walkability assessments. All indicators are related to policy-amenable aspects of the microscale pedestrian environment and ultimately aim to support the design of more healthy and sustainable cities.

 How you calculated the microscale Walkability Scores presented in the map?

The walkability attractiveness values per each cell in the map describe the density of segments weighted by a microscale walkability score (WS). These scores were calculated in the raw GIS files per each polyline feature by applying the following formula:

WS=SUM(S1_1+S2_1+S2_2+S3_1+S3_2+S4_1+S5_1+S5_2+S6_1+S7_1+S8_1+S8_2+S9_1+S10_1+S1 1_1+S12_1+S12_2+S13_1+S14_1+S14_2+C11_1+C12_1+C12_2+C13_1)/31

Where WS is the microscale walkability score, S#_# & C##_# are the codes of each individual rated item, and 31 is the maximum number of points that a segment can collect (note: each segment can take 0, 1, or 2 points per audit item).
